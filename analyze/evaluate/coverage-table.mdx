A coverage table provides a structured matrix view of your insurance coverage, showing what you have, in how much coverage, and on what terms. This view makes it easy to understand your complete coverage picture, identify gaps, and verify program adequacy.

## Understanding Coverage Tables

A coverage table extracts key coverage information from one or more insurance policies and presents it in a structured format. Each row represents a coverage line (General Liability, Property, etc.), and each column represents a key data point (limits, deductibles, carriers, etc.).

A coverage table helps you:

- **See your complete program at a glance** - Understand all coverage lines and key terms
- **Identify coverage gaps** - Spot missing coverages or inadequate limits
- **Compare across policies** - Review how coverage differs across carriers, locations, or years
- **Verify adequacy** - Check whether your coverage meets contractual or regulatory requirements
- **Track endorsements** - See which endorsements apply to each coverage
- **Manage renewals** - Track what needs to be renewed and on what terms

## What Gets Extracted

A coverage table includes:

- **Coverage line** - The type of coverage (General Liability, Property, Auto, Professional Liability, etc.)
- **Carrier** - Which company is providing coverage
- **Policy number** - For reference and tracking
- **Limits of liability** - Maximum coverage amount
- **Deductibles** - What you pay first
- **Self-insured retentions (SIRs)** - What the insured retains
- **Aggregates** - Limits on total claims per year
- **Coverage territory** - Geographic scope (US, US & Canada, Worldwide, etc.)
- **Key endorsements** - Notable modifications or enhancements
- **Special conditions** - Any unusual terms or requirements
- **Period** - Effective and expiration dates

## When to Use a Coverage Table

Use a coverage table when you need to:

- **Conduct a coverage adequacy review** - Verify that coverage limits are appropriate for your risk
- **Present program overview** - Show stakeholders (CFO, audit committee, clients) your complete coverage picture
- **Support renewal planning** - Track what's in place and what needs attention
- **Perform gap analysis** - Identify missing coverages before a claim reveals the gap
- **Verify compliance** - Confirm that your policies meet contractual, regulatory, or lender requirements
- **Manage a complex program** - Organize coverage across multiple locations, subsidiaries, or business units
- **Support claims coordination** - Quickly identify which policy applies to a loss
- **Track changes over time** - Compare year-to-year coverage to understand program evolution

## How to Read a Coverage Table

A typical coverage table looks like this:

| Coverage Line | Carrier | Limits | Deductible | SIR | Territory | Key Endorsements | Expiration |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| General Liability | Carrier A | $1M/$2M | $5K | — | US & Canada | None | 12/31/25 |
| Products Liability | Carrier A | $1M | $5K | — | US & Canada | None | 12/31/25 |
| Property | Carrier B | $5M | $50K | — | US & Canada | Business Interruption | 06/30/26 |
| Commercial Auto | Carrier C | $1M | $1K | — | US | Hired & Non-Owned | 03/31/26 |
| Professional Liability | Carrier D | $500K | $10K | $10K | US | Claims Made tail endorsement | 09/30/25 |

**Reading the table:**
- Each row is a coverage line
- Key information is organized in columns
- Gaps appear as missing rows (e.g., no Employment Practices Liability shown)
- Differences in terms stand out (e.g., different carriers, deductibles, or limits)

## Using Coverage Tables for Gap Analysis

A coverage table makes it easy to spot gaps:

- **Missing coverage lines** - Rows with nothing listed (e.g., no umbrella liability shown)
- **Inadequate limits** - Limits that are too low for your risk (compare to contractual requirements or industry benchmarks)
- **High deductibles** - Deductibles that require too much out-of-pocket exposure
- **Geographic gaps** - Territory limitations that don't match your operations (e.g., coverage limited to US but you have Canadian operations)
- **Endorsement gaps** - Missing endorsements that should apply (e.g., no additional insured endorsements for your clients)
- **Expiration tracking** - Policies expiring at different times creating renewal coordination challenges

## Output Organization

A coverage table is organized clearly for quick reference:

- **By coverage type** - All liability coverages grouped together, all property coverages grouped, etc.
- **By policy** - Each column represents one policy
- **By carrier** - Each row represents coverage from one carrier (useful for comparing carriers)
- **By business unit** - Each section represents one location or subsidiary (useful for program management)

## Next Steps

Once you've created a coverage table:

- **Perform gap analysis** - Identify coverage gaps and develop a plan to address them
- **Verify compliance** - Compare your coverage against contractual or regulatory requirements
- **Plan renewals** - Use the table to organize renewal discussions with brokers and carriers
