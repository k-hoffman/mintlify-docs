# Coverage Determination

Coverage determination is the process of evaluating whether a specific claim or loss scenario is covered under an insurance policy. Qumis Legal Search accelerates this process by helping you find case law, statutory authority, and regulatory guidance relevant to your specific coverage question.

---

## The coverage determination workflow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Frame the coverage question">
    Start by defining the precise question you need to answer. Good coverage questions are specific: "Does a CGL policy's 'your work' exclusion apply to damage caused by a subcontractor's defective installation?" is more effective than "Is construction defect covered?"

    Common question patterns:
    - Does the insuring agreement respond to these facts?
    - Does exclusion [X] apply to this scenario?
    - How is [defined term] interpreted in [jurisdiction]?
    - Does the [endorsement/exception] restore coverage that was excluded?
  </Step>
  <Step title="Analyze the policy language">
    Before researching legal authority, understand what the policy says. Use Qumis to analyze the specific provisions:
    - "What does the insuring agreement cover and what conditions must be met?"
    - "What exclusions could apply to [claim facts]?"
    - "Are there exceptions to the applicable exclusions?"

    → [Policy Analysis](/analyze/summarize/policy-analysis) | [Claims Analysis](/analyze/evaluate/claims-analysis)
  </Step>
  <Step title="Research legal authority">
    Use Legal Search to find how courts interpret the specific policy language at issue. Focus on:
    - Cases in the governing jurisdiction interpreting identical or similar language
    - Cases involving similar fact patterns
    - Jurisdictional trends (is there a majority vs. minority rule?)
    - Recent decisions that may signal shifts in interpretation
  </Step>
  <Step title="Synthesize your determination">
    Combine the policy analysis and legal research into a documented determination. Structure it as: facts → policy analysis → legal authority → conclusion.

    → [Building Defensible Determinations](/analyze/evaluate/building-defensible-determinations)
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Effective Legal Search prompts for coverage questions

**Insuring agreement interpretation:**
- "Find cases interpreting the phrase 'sudden and accidental' in CGL pollution exclusion exceptions."
- "How do courts define 'occurrence' in the context of [construction defect / intentional act / ongoing damage]?"

**Exclusion applicability:**
- "Find authority on whether the professional services exclusion in a CGL policy applies to [specific activity]."
- "How have courts applied the 'expected or intended' exclusion to claims involving [negligence / recklessness / knowledge of defect]?"

**Trigger and allocation:**
- "What trigger theory do courts in [jurisdiction] apply to [occurrence-based / claims-made] policies for [latent / progressive] losses?"
- "Find cases addressing allocation of coverage across multiple policy periods for continuous property damage."

**Endorsement and exception interpretation:**
- "How have courts interpreted the 'additional insured — ongoing operations' endorsement (ISO CG 20 10) in the context of completed operations claims?"

---

## Jurisdiction-specific research

Coverage law varies significantly by jurisdiction. When researching, specify your jurisdiction:

*"In Texas, how do courts interpret the 'eight corners rule' for determining the duty to defend?"*

*"Find New York case law on the application of the pollution exclusion to indoor air quality claims."*

*"What is California's approach to allocating coverage between primary and excess carriers for progressive losses?"*

---

## Organizing your research

As you build your coverage research, organize findings into three categories:

**Supporting authority.** Cases and statutes that support your coverage position.

**Opposing authority.** Cases and statutes that could support the opposing position. A thorough determination addresses these rather than ignoring them.

**Distinguishable authority.** Cases that appear relevant but involve materially different facts, policy language, or jurisdictional law.
