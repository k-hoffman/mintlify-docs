This workflow is for situations where you need more than a coverage determination — you need a persuasive, well-documented coverage argument. Whether you're drafting a coverage opinion, preparing for mediation, supporting outside counsel, or building a position for regulatory response, this guide shows how to use Qumis to build an evidence-based case.

---

## When you need to build a case

**Coverage disputes.** You've made a coverage determination, but the other party disagrees. You need to build a persuasive argument supported by policy language and legal authority.

**Complex claims.** The coverage question involves multiple policies, multiple exclusions, or novel fact patterns. You need to construct a thorough, well-organized analysis.

**Client advisory.** A client needs a coverage opinion they can rely on for a business decision — whether to self-insure, whether to accept a settlement, whether to tender to another carrier.

**Regulatory or litigation support.** You're supporting outside counsel or responding to a regulatory inquiry. The work product needs to withstand professional scrutiny.

## The case-building workflow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Define the position">
    Start with the conclusion you're evaluating (not assuming). What coverage position are you assessing? Be specific: "Whether the policy's professional services exclusion bars coverage for the insured's failure to properly install a security system, where the installation involved both professional design and manual labor."
  </Step>
  <Step title="Build the policy foundation">
    Establish what the policy says using Qumis policy analysis:
    - Identify every relevant provision (insuring agreements, exclusions, conditions, definitions)
    - Map how the provisions interact
    - Document the chain from coverage grant through exclusions and exceptions
    - Identify any ambiguities in the policy language
  </Step>
  <Step title="Research primary authority">
    Use Legal Search to find the strongest legal authority:
    - Cases in the governing jurisdiction
    - Cases interpreting identical or substantially similar policy language
    - Cases with fact patterns closest to yours
    - Recent appellate decisions
  </Step>
  <Step title="Research secondary authority">
    Broaden your research to strengthen the argument:
    - Treatises and secondary sources discussing the legal issue
    - Regulatory guidance or bulletins
    - Industry standards or practices
    - Majority/minority rule analysis across jurisdictions
  </Step>
  <Step title="Anticipate counterarguments">
    Ask Qumis to identify potential counterarguments:
    - "What arguments would the opposing side make against our coverage position?"
    - "Are there cases reaching the opposite conclusion, and how are they distinguishable?"
    - "What factual distinctions could weaken our position?"

    Address each counterargument in your case document. This is what makes the difference between a persuasive argument and a one-sided brief.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Assemble the case document">
    Organize your analysis into a structured document:
    1. **Statement of facts** — the relevant facts giving rise to the coverage question
    2. **Policy analysis** — the relevant provisions and how they apply
    3. **Legal authority** — supporting case law and statutory authority
    4. **Counterargument analysis** — opposing positions and why they don't apply
    5. **Conclusion** — your coverage position with confidence level
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Qumis prompts for case building

**Policy foundation:**
- "Identify every provision in this policy that is relevant to a claim involving [facts]. Include insuring agreements, exclusions, conditions, and definitions."
- "Trace the coverage path: from the insuring agreement through applicable exclusions, exceptions, and conditions. Is there a break in the coverage chain?"

**Legal research:**
- "Find the three strongest cases supporting the position that [coverage position]."
- "Find cases where courts have rejected the argument that [opposing position]."
- "What is the current split of authority on [legal issue], and which position do courts in [jurisdiction] follow?"

**Counterargument identification:**
- "What are the three strongest arguments against our position that [coverage position]?"
- "How would opposing counsel distinguish the cases we're relying on?"

**Document assembly:**
- "Draft a coverage opinion following this structure: facts, policy analysis, legal authority, counterargument analysis, conclusion."
- "Summarize our position in two paragraphs suitable for an executive briefing."

---

## Quality standards

A strong coverage case meets these criteria:

**Grounded in policy text.** Every coverage conclusion traces back to specific policy language, cited by section and provision.

**Supported by authority.** Key interpretive points are supported by case law or other legal authority, preferably from the governing jurisdiction.

**Addresses counterarguments.** The analysis acknowledges and responds to the strongest opposing arguments.

**Clearly reasoned.** The logical path from facts through analysis to conclusion is explicit and easy to follow.

**Appropriately qualified.** Where the law is unsettled or the facts are ambiguous, the analysis acknowledges uncertainty rather than overstating confidence.
