Policy summarization turns a dense insurance document into actionable intelligence. Whether you're preparing for a client meeting, triaging a claim, or evaluating coverage adequacy, Qumis reads the entire policy—including all endorsements and schedules—and produces a clear, structured analysis you can use immediately.

---

## When to summarize

Summarize a policy whenever you need rapid clarity on what's actually covered. Use it for new submissions to identify improvement opportunities, pre-meeting preparation to understand key provisions before conversations begin, coverage adequacy reviews to systematically evaluate whether the policy meets the insured's risk profile, onboarding new accounts where you need to understand the existing program fast, or claims triage to understand coverage provisions when a claim arrives. You can also use policy summaries to prepare client-ready letters, position your advisory value, or brief colleagues and counsel who need to understand coverage without reading the full document.

## How it works

<Steps>
  <Step title="Upload or Select the Policy">
    Select the insurance policy you want to analyze. Provide the base policy document along with all endorsements, amendments, and related coverage documents. You can upload or select from your existing library.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Run the Analysis">
    Choose your approach: use the conversational Summarize capability in Chat for exploratory analysis and scenario-based questions, or use the Single Policy Analysis Report feature for a structured, formatted document. Both read the entire policy and extract the same core information.

  <Step title="Get Your Summary">
    Receive a structured summary covering coverage grants, limits, deductibles, exclusions, conditions, endorsements, and defined terms. For Chat analysis, you can follow up with targeted questions. For a formal report, you get a polished document ready for clients, claims files, or team reference.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Generate a Report (Optional)">

  <Step title="Review and Customize">
    Review the output. For Chat summaries, ask clarifying questions or request plain-language explanations. For formal reports, edit sections, add your own insights, adjust emphasis, include recommendations, or compare with prior policies.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Export and Share">
    Export your report in PDF, Word, or HTML format. Share with clients during meetings, include in claims files, circulate internally for team reference, or attach to proposals and board presentations.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## What Qumis extracts

Qumis reads through the entire policy—every page, endorsement, schedule, and defined term—and identifies what matters: the coverage overview showing what the policy covers, who is insured, and the key insuring agreements. It pulls out all limits and deductibles, including per-occurrence limits, aggregates, sublimits by coverage type, and deductible structures. The system flags key exclusions, particularly those that are broader or narrower than standard market forms, explaining their practical impact. Qumis also captures conditions and requirements like notice provisions, cooperation clauses, and cancellation terms that could affect coverage. Every endorsement is analyzed to show how it modifies the base policy—which coverages it adds, restricts, or eliminates. Finally, Qumis identifies defined terms that control the scope of coverage, especially where they differ from standard definitions in the market.

## What you get

You have two output options depending on your workflow. **Chat-based summaries** are conversational: ask questions, get answers, drill into specific provisions, explore scenarios. This approach is best for exploration and understanding—you can request client-ready language, ask "what if" questions, and get targeted responses. **Single Policy Analysis Reports** are structured, formatted documents suitable for client presentations, claims files, team reference, or professional documentation. Reports include an executive summary for quick reference, policy basics (named insured, period, form numbers), detailed coverage analysis for each coverage part, limits and deductibles in an easy-to-reference table, key exclusions with explanations, every endorsement listed and explained, important conditions and definitions, notable provisions unique to the policy, a risk assessment evaluating coverage adequacy, and recommendations for improvements. You can configure report depth (executive, standard, or detailed), choose which sections to include, and emphasize specific focus areas before generation.

## Tips for your role

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Brokers & Producers">
    Use summaries to quickly understand prospect policies and identify top coverage gaps for your pitch meeting. Before any client meeting, run a quick analysis to refresh your understanding of their coverage, focusing on areas the client is likely to ask about. Generate client-ready policy analysis reports for new business presentations to demonstrate expertise and positioning.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Account Managers">
    Analyze the current policy before renewal begins to identify provisions needing renegotiation and coverage gaps to address. Use summaries as foundations for coverage letters documenting what the policy covers and recommending improvements. Provide clients with policy summaries during onboarding and renewal prep to demonstrate advisory value and understanding.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Claims Professionals">
    When a new claim arrives, run a policy analysis focused on relevant coverage provisions—this gives you a starting point for coverage determination. Ask Qumis to map claim facts against specific policy provisions (insuring agreement, exclusions, conditions) to build your analysis. Use comprehensive summaries as part of your claims file for internal reference and claims coordination.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Risk Managers">
    Analyze each policy in your portfolio individually, then use findings to assess overall program adequacy. Request executive-level summaries of each major policy for board presentations (two paragraphs maximum, focusing on coverage, key limits, and significant gaps). Use policy summaries to understand program components, identify gaps or redundancies, and support strategic planning.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>
