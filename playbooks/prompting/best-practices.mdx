The quality of your Qumis output depends heavily on how you frame your questions. This page covers the prompting techniques that experienced users rely on — from foundational principles to power user patterns.

---

## The prompt formula

Every strong prompt follows the same structure:

**Role + Action + Document + Purpose**

> You're a senior broker helping renew a client. Compare these two quotes focusing on liability limits for my client proposal.

This formula works because it gives Qumis everything it needs to deliver a useful answer: who you are, what you want done, which documents to analyze, and how you'll use the output.

| Component    | What it does                                    | Example                                                       |
| ------------ | ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Role**     | Sets your perspective and expertise level        | "You're a junior producer," "You're a senior claims adjuster" |
| **Action**   | Tells Qumis what to do                          | "Compare," "Summarize," "Identify gaps in"                    |
| **Document** | Points to the documents you want analyzed        | "these two quotes," "this CGL policy," "the attached binder"  |
| **Purpose**  | Explains how you'll use the output               | "for my client proposal," "for a board presentation," "to prepare for a renewal meeting" |

You don't need all four every time — but the more context you provide, the more targeted and useful the analysis will be.

## Six rules for better prompts

### 1. Be specific

Name the coverage, limit, or section you need. Vague questions get vague answers.

| Instead of...                  | Try...                                                                                                                         |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| "What does this policy say?"   | "Summarize the key limits, deductibles, and exclusions in this policy"                                                          |
| "Compare these"                | "Compare these quotes side-by-side, focusing on coverage differences"                                                           |
| "Is this covered?"             | "Does this policy cover water damage? Cite the relevant language"                                                               |
| "Help with this claim"         | "Based on the policy, is this slip-and-fall likely covered?"                                                                    |
| "Is this good coverage?"       | "What are the most significant coverage gaps in this property policy for a restaurant with outdoor seating?"                    |

### 2. State your goal

Tell Qumis what you're trying to accomplish. A prompt with purpose produces better analysis than a generic question.

- _"I'm preparing a renewal recommendation for my client. Summarize the key changes between the expiring policy and this renewal quote."_
- _"I need to check whether our client's insurance meets the requirements in this contract. Map each requirement against the current coverage."_

### 3. Request the format

Tell Qumis how you want the output structured. This saves you reformatting time and ensures you get actionable output.

- _"Format as a table with columns for: exclusion name, policy section, what it excludes, and any exceptions that might restore coverage."_
- _"Give me a two-paragraph executive summary suitable for a client email."_
- _"List the differences as bullet points, grouped by coverage type."_

### 4. Use insurance terms

Qumis is built for insurance professionals and understands industry language. You don't need to simplify your terminology — use the same language you'd use with a colleague.

- _"Does this CGL policy provide products-completed operations coverage?"_
- _"Is the professional liability coverage claims-made or occurrence, and what is the retroactive date?"_
- _"What are the sublimits and SIRs under the cyber liability section?"_

### 5. Ask follow-up questions

Treat the first response as a starting point. The most valuable insights often come from your second and third questions.

- **Start broad:** "Summarize the key coverage provisions in this cyber policy."
- **Then narrow:** "Focus on the social engineering fraud coverage. What are the sublimits and conditions?"
- **Then apply:** "If an employee fell for a phishing email that resulted in a \$500K wire transfer, would this policy respond?"

### 6. Name and save your prompt

When a prompt works well, save it to your [Prompt Library](/platform/prompt-library) with a clear, descriptive name. "Extract CGL Sublimits" is easier to find than "Policy Question" — and a well-named library makes your team more efficient over time.

## Quick starters

Not sure where to begin? These prompts work across most document types and cover the most common workflows:

- _"Summarize the key terms of this policy, including limits, deductibles, and major exclusions."_
- _"Compare these documents, highlighting differences in coverage, limits, and conditions."_
- _"Does this policy meet the insurance requirements in this contract?"_
- _"Explain [coverage type] in plain language for my client."_
- _"What exclusions apply to [specific situation]?"_
- _"Identify any sublimits or deductibles that apply to [specific coverage]."_

## Set the scenario

Beyond the formula, one of the most powerful techniques is giving Qumis business context. When you describe the specific situation, the AI focuses on what matters for your case — not just what's in the document.

_"My client is a mid-size general contractor that subcontracts 60% of its work. Review this CGL policy and identify any provisions that could create coverage gaps for subcontractor-related claims."_

_"We're preparing for a renewal meeting with a manufacturing client who had two product liability claims last year. Summarize how this renewal quote addresses their loss history."_

The more Qumis understands about the insured, the risk, and the business context, the more relevant the analysis becomes.

## Tailor output with a reference file

When you need Qumis to match a specific format — a coverage summary template, an internal spreadsheet layout, or a checklist your team already uses — upload the reference file alongside your documents and point to it in your prompt.

_"Refer to the Excel file named 'Coverage_Summary_Template.xlsx' as the model. Create a summary table for these policies using the same column headers and layout."_

This technique is especially useful for:

- **Internal templates** — match your firm's coverage summary format without re-creating it from scratch
- **Client deliverables** — produce output that fits directly into an existing report or proposal
- **Consistency across your team** — everyone produces the same structured output by referencing the same template

<Tip>
  When referencing a template, be explicit about what to replicate: column headers, row structure, formatting style, and what content to include in each field. The more precise your instructions, the closer the output will match your template.
</Tip>

## Prompts by document type

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Policies">
    - "What are the key exclusions that could affect [specific claim scenario]?"
    - "Walk me through the coverage trigger — is this occurrence-based or claims-made, and what are the implications?"
    - "Identify any sublimits or deductibles that apply to [specific coverage]."
    - "Does this policy have a duty to defend, and are defense costs inside or outside the limits?"
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Quotes and proposals">
    - "Compare the limits structure, deductibles, and key exclusions between these two quotes."
    - "What coverage is Quote A offering that Quote B is not?"
    - "Which quote provides better coverage for [specific risk scenario], and why?"
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Endorsements">
    - "How does this endorsement modify the base policy's coverage for [specific claim type]?"
    - "Does this endorsement broaden or restrict coverage, and in what specific ways?"
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Contracts with insurance requirements">
    - "Does the insurance program described in [attached policy] meet the requirements specified in Section [X] of this contract?"
    - "What coverage gaps exist between the contractual requirements and the actual policy?"
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Claims documents">
    - "Based on this reservation of rights letter, what coverage defenses is the carrier raising?"
    - "Given the facts in this claim report, which policy provisions are most relevant to the coverage determination?"
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Common mistakes

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Asking yes/no questions when you need analysis">
    Instead of "Does this policy cover cyber risks?", ask "How does this policy address cyber risks, and what are the key limitations?" Open-ended questions produce more useful analysis than binary ones.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Not providing enough context">
    Qumis doesn't know your client's industry, risk profile, or priorities unless you tell it. Adding even one sentence of context — "My client is a restaurant chain with 12 locations" — dramatically improves output relevance.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Accepting the first response without follow-up">
    The iterative dialogue is where the real value emerges. Your second and third questions can push deeper on the findings that matter most and uncover details the initial summary might not surface.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Asking about information not in the document">
    Qumis analyzes the documents you provide. If you need market context, pricing benchmarks, or information from documents that aren't uploaded, those answers are outside the scope of the analysis.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Writing prompts that are too short">
    A one-line prompt like "Review this policy" gives Qumis no direction. Add your role, the specific question, and how you'll use the output. Even two or three extra sentences of context can significantly improve the response.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Tips for your role

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Brokers & Producers">
    **Competitive quoting:** When comparing quotes, specify the client's primary risks and ask Qumis to evaluate each quote against those specific exposures. This produces analysis you can share directly with clients.

    **Pre-meeting prep:** Before a client meeting, ask Qumis to summarize the key coverage features and any notable limitations. Request the output in a format you can reference during the conversation.

    **Proposal support:** Ask Qumis to draft coverage comparison language you can include in proposals. Specify the audience (CFO, risk manager, business owner) for appropriately tailored language.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Account Managers">
    **Renewal preparation:** Start with "Compare the expiring policy against this renewal quote and highlight every change — additions, removals, and modifications to limits, deductibles, and exclusions."

    **Contract reviews:** When checking client contracts, ask Qumis to identify every insurance requirement and map it against the current coverage program.

    **Client advisory:** Use Qumis to identify coverage gaps, then ask it to explain each gap in plain language suitable for a coverage letter to the client.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Claims Professionals">
    **Coverage analysis:** Frame questions around the specific facts of the claim: "Given [specific facts], does the insuring agreement in Section [X] respond?"

    **Exclusion analysis:** Ask Qumis to work through each potentially applicable exclusion: "Are there any exclusions that could bar coverage for [specific claim], and do any exceptions to those exclusions apply?"

    **Legal research integration:** Combine Qumis policy analysis with Legal Search: "What does the policy say about [issue]?" then "Find case law interpreting similar policy language."
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Risk Managers">
    **Compliance verification:** Ask Qumis to check your insurance program against contractual requirements: "Review this lease and identify every insurance requirement, then check whether our current policy meets each one."

    **Exposure analysis:** Ask about coverage adequacy for specific risk scenarios: "If we experienced [scenario], how would our current insurance program respond? Walk through each relevant policy."

    **Board reporting:** Request executive-level summaries: "Summarize our cyber insurance coverage in two paragraphs, including limits, key exclusions, and the most significant gaps, suitable for a board presentation."
  </Tab>
</Tabs>
