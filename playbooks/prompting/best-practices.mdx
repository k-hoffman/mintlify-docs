# Tips and Best Practices

The quality of your Qumis output depends heavily on how you frame your questions. This page covers the prompting techniques that experienced users rely on — from foundational principles to power user patterns.

---

## The golden rule: be specific

Vague questions get vague answers. Specific questions get useful analysis.

| Instead of...                  | Try...                                                                                                                         |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| "What does this policy cover?" | "Does this CGL policy cover product liability claims arising from manufacturing defects?"                                      |
| "Compare these policies"       | "Compare the professional liability coverage in these two quotes, focusing on prior acts coverage and defense cost provisions" |
| "Is this good coverage?"       | "What are the most significant coverage gaps in this property policy for a restaurant with outdoor seating?"                   |

The more context you provide about what you're looking for and why, the more targeted and useful the analysis will be.

---

## Five prompting techniques

### 1. Set the scenario

Tell Qumis the business context. This helps the AI focus on what matters for your specific situation.

_"My client is a mid-size general contractor that subcontracts 60% of its work. Review this CGL policy and identify any provisions that could create coverage gaps for subcontractor-related claims."_

### 2. Ask follow-up questions

Treat the first response as a draft. The most valuable insights often come from your second and third questions.

- **Start broad:** "Summarize the key coverage provisions in this cyber policy."
- **Then narrow:** "Focus on the social engineering fraud coverage. What are the sublimits and conditions?"
- **Then apply:** "If an employee fell for a phishing email that resulted in a \$500K wire transfer, would this policy respond?"

### 3. Request a specific format

Tell Qumis how you want the output structured. This saves you reformatting time and ensures you get actionable output.

_"List the exclusions in this policy that could affect a construction defect claim. Format as a table with columns for: exclusion name, policy section, what it excludes, and any exceptions that might restore coverage."_

### 4. Compare against a standard

Give the AI a benchmark to evaluate against.

_"Compare this Workers' Comp policy against standard guaranteed cost provisions. Flag any non-standard exclusions, unusual conditions, or deviations from typical guaranteed cost structure."_

### 5. Request the executive version

When you need to share findings with a client or colleague who doesn't want the full analysis.

_"Draft a two-paragraph summary of the key differences between these quotes that I can include in an email to my client. Focus on the three most material coverage differences."_

## Prompts by document type

### Policies

- "What are the key exclusions that could affect [specific claim scenario]?"
- "Walk me through the coverage trigger — is this occurrence-based or claims-made, and what are the implications?"
- "Identify any sublimits or deductibles that apply to [specific coverage]."
- "Does this policy have a duty to defend, and are defense costs inside or outside the limits?"

### Quotes and proposals

- "Compare the limits structure, deductibles, and key exclusions between these two quotes."
- "What coverage is Quote A offering that Quote B is not?"
- "Which quote provides better coverage for [specific risk scenario], and why?"

### Endorsements

- "How does this endorsement modify the base policy's coverage for [specific claim type]?"
- "Does this endorsement broaden or restrict coverage, and in what specific ways?"

### Contracts with insurance requirements

- "Does the insurance program described in [attached policy] meet the requirements specified in Section [X] of this contract?"
- "What coverage gaps exist between the contractual requirements and the actual policy?"

### Claims documents

- "Based on this reservation of rights letter, what coverage defenses is the carrier raising?"
- "Given the facts in this claim report, which policy provisions are most relevant to the coverage determination?"

## Common mistakes

**Asking yes/no questions when you need analysis.** Instead of "Does this policy cover cyber risks?", ask "How does this policy address cyber risks, and what are the key limitations?"

**Not providing enough context.** The AI doesn't know your client's industry, risk profile, or priorities unless you tell it. Context dramatically improves output relevance.

**Accepting the first response without follow-up.** The iterative dialogue is where the real value emerges. Push deeper on the findings that matter most.

**Asking about information not in the document.** Qumis analyzes the documents you provide. If you need market context or pricing benchmarks, that's outside the scope of document analysis.

## Tips for your role

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Brokers & Producers">
    **Competitive quoting:** When comparing quotes, specify the client's primary risks and ask Qumis to evaluate each quote against those specific exposures. This produces analysis you can share directly with clients.

    **Pre-meeting prep:** Before a client meeting, ask Qumis to summarize the key coverage features and any notable limitations. Request the output in a format you can reference during the conversation.

    **Proposal support:** Ask Qumis to draft coverage comparison language you can include in proposals. Specify the audience (CFO, risk manager, business owner) for appropriately tailored language.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Account Managers">
    **Renewal preparation:** Start with "Compare the expiring policy against this renewal quote and highlight every change — additions, removals, and modifications to limits, deductibles, and exclusions."

    **Contract reviews:** When checking client contracts, ask Qumis to identify every insurance requirement and map it against the current coverage program.

    **Client advisory:** Use Qumis to identify coverage gaps, then ask it to explain each gap in plain language suitable for a coverage letter to the client.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Claims Professionals">
    **Coverage analysis:** Frame questions around the specific facts of the claim: "Given [specific facts], does the insuring agreement in Section [X] respond?"

    **Exclusion analysis:** Ask Qumis to work through each potentially applicable exclusion: "Are there any exclusions that could bar coverage for [specific claim], and do any exceptions to those exclusions apply?"

    **Legal research integration:** Combine Qumis policy analysis with Legal Search: "What does the policy say about [issue]?" then "Find case law interpreting similar policy language."
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Risk Managers">
    **Compliance verification:** Ask Qumis to check your insurance program against contractual requirements: "Review this lease and identify every insurance requirement, then check whether our current policy meets each one."

    **Exposure analysis:** Ask about coverage adequacy for specific risk scenarios: "If we experienced [scenario], how would our current insurance program respond? Walk through each relevant policy."

    **Board reporting:** Request executive-level summaries: "Summarize our cyber insurance coverage in two paragraphs, including limits, key exclusions, and the most significant gaps, suitable for a board presentation."
  </Tab>
</Tabs>
