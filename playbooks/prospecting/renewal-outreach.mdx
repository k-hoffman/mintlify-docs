## The strategy

Most producers approach renewal-timed outreach the same way: identify a prospect whose policy is coming up for renewal, call or email them, and ask for the chance to quote. The problem is that every other broker in town is doing the exact same thing. The prospect hears "let us quote it" from three to five brokers every cycle. It's noise.

Qumis lets you lead with something different: a point of view. Instead of asking for the expiring, you show up with intelligence about what programs in the prospect's industry and class of business typically look like, where coverage gaps commonly appear, and what questions they should be asking their current broker. You're not selling — you're demonstrating expertise before they've given you anything.

This shifts the dynamic from "convince me to switch" to "I need to understand what you're seeing."

---

## Prerequisites

<Info>
  This playbook works best when you have a defined target list of prospects by industry vertical. If you don't have one yet, start by picking the two or three verticals where you have the deepest expertise and build from there.
</Info>

Before running this playbook, you should have:

- A target prospect list with estimated renewal dates (or at least renewal quarters)
- Access to Qumis with documents uploaded for the relevant verticals (sample policies, market benchmarks, or policies from similar accounts in your book)
- Familiarity with Chats, reports, and Legal Search features

## The Qumis workflow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Build vertical intelligence in Document Vault">
    For each target vertical (construction, healthcare, manufacturing, real estate, tech/cyber, etc.), create a folder in the Document Vault and upload representative policies from that class of business. These can come from your existing book, sample forms, or publicly available policy specimens.

    The goal is to give Qumis a corpus of documents in the prospect's vertical so you can generate class-specific intelligence, not generic talking points.

    **Example folder structure:**
    ```
    Prospecting Intelligence/
    ├── Construction/
    │   ├── sample-GL-policy-construction.pdf
    │   ├── sample-builders-risk.pdf
    │   └── wrap-up-program-specimen.pdf
    ├── Healthcare/
    │   ├── sample-medical-malpractice.pdf
    │   └── sample-D&O-healthcare.pdf
    └── Manufacturing/
        ├── sample-product-liability.pdf
        └── sample-pollution-liability.pdf
    ```

    <Warning>
      Never upload a current client's policy and share the analysis with a prospect. Use specimen policies, sample forms, or anonymized documents. This playbook is about demonstrating class-of-business expertise, not sharing confidential client data.
    </Warning>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Generate a coverage intelligence brief using Chats">
    Open a Chat session with the vertical's documents and prompt Qumis to produce a coverage intelligence brief. This is the core asset you'll use in outreach.

    **Example prompt:**
    ```
    Based on these commercial general liability policies for construction
    risks, identify the five most common coverage gaps, exclusions, or
    areas of ambiguity that could create exposure for a mid-size
    general contractor. For each finding, explain: (1) what the gap is,
    (2) why it matters operationally, and (3) what questions the insured
    should be asking their broker. Cite specific policy language.
    ```

    The output gives you a one-page intelligence brief that demonstrates deep vertical expertise without requiring the prospect's actual documents.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Add legal context with Legal Search">
    For each of the top findings in your coverage intelligence brief, run a Legal Search query to find relevant case law where that coverage gap actually resulted in a denied claim or coverage dispute.

    **Example query:**
    ```
    Cases where a commercial general liability pollution exclusion
    was applied to deny coverage for a construction site
    contamination claim
    ```

    Adding a real-world case reference to your outreach transforms it from "here's what we think" to "here's what happened when this gap wasn't caught." That's a fundamentally different level of credibility.

    <Tip>
      You don't need to cite every case in your outreach email. Pick one strong example per finding. The goal is to make the prospect think "I should check whether my program has this issue," not to overwhelm them with legal research.
    </Tip>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Package the brief into a report">
    Generate a report from your findings. Keep it tight: a one- to two-page summary with the top three findings, each supported by a brief legal reference. This is not a comprehensive policy review — it's a teaser designed to earn a meeting.

    Structure the report as:
    1. **Industry context** — two to three sentences on the vertical and common program structures
    2. **Top coverage considerations** — three findings with policy language citations
    3. **Why this matters** — one case reference or real-world consequence per finding
    4. **Suggested next step** — offer to run their actual program through a full analysis
  </Step>

  <Step title="Send the outreach">
    Use the coverage intelligence brief as the centerpiece of your outreach. Don't attach the full report in the first email — reference the findings and offer the report as the next step.

    Time your outreach to land 90–120 days before the prospect's estimated renewal date. This gives them time to act but creates enough proximity to renewal that switching brokers feels actionable.
  </Step>
</Steps>

    > "We use a platform called Qumis. It's built by insurance attorneys and it does structured policy analysis with citations back to the actual policy language. Think of it like having an extra coverage attorney review your program. We've been finding things that standard broker reviews miss."

    **If they say "we're happy with our broker":**
    > "That's great. This isn't about replacing anyone. The brief is just a checklist of things worth confirming with your current team. If everything checks out, you'll have peace of mind. If something doesn't, you'll catch it before a claim."
  </Tab>

## Building your outreach cadence

Here's the recommended cadence for a single prospect:

| Touchpoint | Timing | Channel | Content |
|---|---|---|---|
| First touch | 120 days pre-renewal | Email | Lead with top finding, offer the brief |
| Follow-up 1 | 110 days pre-renewal | Email | Resend with different angle or second finding |
| Follow-up 2 | 100 days pre-renewal | Phone | Reference the emails, offer to walk through the brief |
| Follow-up 3 | 90 days pre-renewal | LinkedIn | Short message, offer the brief |
| Final touch | 75 days pre-renewal | Email | Last chance framing: "wanted to make sure you had this before renewal kicks off" |

<Note>
  If the prospect engages at any point, shift from the outreach cadence to a discovery conversation. The goal of the brief is to earn the meeting, not to close the deal over email.
</Note>

## Measuring success

| Metric | What to track | Target |
|---|---|---|
| Coverage briefs created | Number of vertical-specific intelligence briefs built | 1 per target vertical |
| Outreach sequences launched | Number of prospects entered into the cadence | 10–20 per quarter per producer |
| Response rate | Percentage of prospects who reply to any touchpoint | 15–25% |
| Meetings booked | First meetings secured from outreach | 3–5 per quarter per producer |
| Pipeline generated | Estimated premium from prospects in active conversations | Track by vertical |

<Tip>
  The coverage intelligence brief is a reusable asset. Once you build one for a vertical, you can use it across every prospect in that class of business. Update it quarterly as you find new patterns or case law.
</Tip>
