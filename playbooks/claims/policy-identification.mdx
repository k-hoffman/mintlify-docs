## The strategy

When a policyholder reports a loss, the instinct is to go straight to the policy that seems most obviously relevant: a slip-and-fall goes to the GL, a fire goes to the property policy, a data breach goes to the cyber policy. But losses don't respect policy boundaries. A single event can trigger coverage under multiple policies simultaneously, and the failure to identify every responding policy can leave significant coverage on the table.

Consider a water damage claim at a commercial building. The property policy covers the physical damage. The GL policy may cover third-party bodily injury or property damage to tenant property. The business interruption coverage on the property policy covers lost income. The umbrella provides excess limits above the primary. An inland marine floater may cover specialized equipment. A pollution policy may respond if the water damage leads to mold contamination. Each policy has its own limits, deductibles, exclusions, and conditions. Missing one means the client either self-insures a loss that was covered or fails to stack limits they're entitled to.

Qumis solves this by letting you load the client's entire program into a single Claims Analysis report and asking which policies respond to the specific loss facts. Instead of analyzing one policy in isolation, you analyze the full program as a coordinated system. The output identifies every policy that potentially applies, explains why, and flags any gaps or conflicts between policies.

This is especially critical for complex losses, multi-party claims, and claims that implicate excess or umbrella layers, where the interplay between policies determines the total available coverage.

## Prerequisites

<Info>
  This playbook is most effective when the client's full program is already organized in the Document Vault. If you've been running the [Broking Playbooks](/playbooks/broking/overview) and [Servicing Playbooks](/playbooks/servicing/overview), it should be. If not, you'll need to gather and upload all active policies before proceeding.
</Info>

Before starting, you should have:

- The client's complete active insurance program (every policy, every endorsement, every layer)
- The facts of the reported loss (even preliminary facts are sufficient for initial identification)
- A Claims Analysis report ready to create

## The Qumis workflow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Load the entire program into a Claims Analysis report">
    Create a new Claims Analysis report and upload every active policy in the client's program. Not just the policy you think applies. All of them.

    **Typical program load:**
    - Commercial general liability (primary)
    - Commercial property (including inland marine, if separate)
    - Commercial auto
    - Workers' compensation / employers' liability
    - Umbrella / excess liability (every layer)
    - Professional liability / E&O
    - Directors & officers liability
    - Employment practices liability
    - Cyber liability
    - Crime / fidelity
    - Pollution liability (if standalone)
    - Any specialty or manuscript policies

    For each policy, include the dec pages, the form, and all endorsements.

    <Tip>
      Loading the full program feels like overkill on a straightforward claim. It isn't. The five minutes it takes to include every policy is nothing compared to the cost of discovering six months later that the inland marine policy had $500K of coverage that nobody checked. Make the full load your default.
    </Tip>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Input the loss facts and request a full-program scan">
    In the report's Chat, provide the loss facts and ask Qumis to evaluate every loaded policy for potential coverage.

    **Full-program identification prompt:**
    ```
    A loss has been reported with the following facts:

    [Insert the loss facts using the same structured format from
    the Coverage Analysis playbook: insured, date of loss, date
    reported, jurisdiction, claim type, detailed narrative,
    estimated loss amount]

    I have loaded the insured's complete active insurance program
    into this report. Analyze EVERY policy in the program and
    determine which policies may provide coverage for this loss.
    For each policy:

    1. POTENTIALLY RESPONSIVE: If the policy may provide coverage,
       explain which insuring agreement is triggered and why.
       Identify the applicable limits, deductibles, and any
       sublimits. Note any exclusions that could apply and whether
       exceptions to those exclusions might restore coverage.

    2. NOT RESPONSIVE: If the policy does not apply to this loss,
       briefly explain why (wrong coverage type, excluded risk,
       wrong policy period, etc.). Keep the explanation to one
       or two sentences.

    3. UNCERTAIN: If coverage is possible but depends on facts
       not yet known or on disputed legal questions, classify
       the policy as uncertain and explain what additional
       information or analysis is needed to make the determination.

    After analyzing each policy individually, assess the program
    as a whole:

    - STACKING: Can limits from multiple policies be stacked for
      this loss? Identify any "other insurance" clauses that
      affect priority and allocation between responding policies.

    - TOWER: If excess/umbrella layers are implicated, map the
      tower showing which primary policies the excess follows
      form to and at what point each layer attaches.

    - GAPS: Are there any exposures created by this loss that
      are not addressed by any policy in the program? Identify
      any coverage holes.

    - CONFLICTS: Are there any conflicting provisions between
      policies (e.g., one policy's "other insurance" clause
      conflicts with another's)?

    Cite specific policy language for every finding.
    ```
  </Step>

  <Step title="Map the coverage tower for the specific loss">
    For losses that are large enough to implicate excess layers, or where multiple policies respond, ask the Chat to produce a visual coverage map specific to this claim.

    **Coverage tower mapping prompt:**
    ```
    Based on the policy identification analysis, produce a coverage
    map for this specific loss that shows:

    1. LAYER STRUCTURE: Which policies respond and in what order,
       from first dollar to the top of the tower. Show the
       attachment point and limit of each layer.

    2. PRIMARY POLICIES: For each primary policy that responds,
       show the applicable per-occurrence/per-claim limit, the
       deductible/SIR, and the remaining aggregate (if known
       or estimable).

    3. EXCESS/UMBRELLA: For each excess layer, show whether it
       follows form to the applicable primary, whether it has
       its own terms for this type of loss, and any exclusions
       that could create a gap in the tower.

    4. TOTAL AVAILABLE COVERAGE: Sum the available limits across
       all responding policies and layers for this loss. Note
       any aggregate erosion, sublimit caps, or "other insurance"
       provisions that reduce the total.

    5. ALLOCATION: If multiple policies respond and the loss
       must be allocated (e.g., between GL and professional
       liability, or between multiple triggered policy periods),
       identify the allocation issue and the applicable rule
       in [jurisdiction].

    Present this as a structured diagram or table that shows the
    complete picture of available coverage for this loss.
    ```
  </Step>

  <Step title="Identify additional insured and third-party coverage">
    For claims involving third parties (contractors, landlords, vendors, joint venture partners), check whether any additional insured provisions extend coverage.

    **Additional insured analysis prompt:**
    ```
    This claim involves the following third parties:
    [List all third parties and their relationship to the insured]

    For each third party, check every policy in the program to
    determine:

    1. Whether the third party qualifies as an additional insured
       under any policy (check blanket AI endorsements, scheduled
       AI endorsements, and any manuscript provisions).

    2. The scope of the additional insured coverage (ongoing
       operations only, completed operations, both, or limited
       to specific activities).

    3. Whether the additional insured coverage is primary and
       noncontributory or excess over the third party's own
       insurance.

    4. Whether any contractual requirement between the insured
       and the third party affects the coverage obligation.

    Cite the specific endorsement language for each finding.
    ```

    <Note>
      Additional insured identification is frequently overlooked and frequently litigated. Getting this right at the outset avoids costly disputes later and ensures that the client meets their contractual obligations. If you've been running the [Contracts and Certificates](/playbooks/servicing/contracts-certificates) servicing playbook, you already have a record of the client's contractual AI obligations that you can reference here.
    </Note>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Document the findings and communicate to the client">
    The Claims Analysis report now contains a complete picture of every policy that may respond to the loss. Use it to guide the claims reporting strategy.

    **Key decisions informed by the policy identification:**

    - **Which carriers to notify:** Every carrier whose policy was identified as "potentially responsive" or "uncertain" should receive a notice of claim or reservation of rights. Late notice to a potentially responding policy is an avoidable error.

    - **Order of coverage:** If multiple primary policies respond, determine which is primary, which is excess, and whether any contribute pro rata.

    - **Reporting sequence:** Report to the primary carrier first, then notify excess layers. For claims that could be large, provide early notice to all layers to preserve rights.

    - **Coordination strategy:** If multiple carriers are involved, consider whether to coordinate the defense and indemnity or allow each carrier to handle its layer independently. Your coverage analysis should inform this strategy.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Policy identification scenarios

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Property loss with business interruption">
    A fire damages a client's manufacturing facility. The obvious policy is commercial property, but the full analysis should check:

    ```
    Identify all policies that may respond to a fire at the
    insured's manufacturing facility. Check for:

    - Property policy: building damage, contents, business
      personal property
    - Business interruption / loss of income (often on the
      property policy, sometimes separate)
    - Extra expense coverage
    - Equipment breakdown (if the fire was caused by or resulted
      in equipment failure)
    - Inland marine (for specialized equipment or goods in transit
      affected by the shutdown)
    - Builders risk (if any portion of the facility was under
      construction or renovation)
    - Environmental/pollution (if the fire released hazardous
      materials)
    - GL (if third parties were injured or their property was
      damaged)
    - Workers' comp (if employees were injured)
    - Umbrella/excess (for GL and possibly employers' liability)
    - Crime (if arson is suspected)

    Analyze each policy and map the full coverage picture.
    ```
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Multi-party construction claim">
    A general contractor faces a claim for property damage at a completed project. The claim involves the GC, two subcontractors, the project owner, and the architect.

    ```
    Identify all policies across the GC's program that may respond
    to this construction defect claim. Also identify coverage
    obligations owed to third parties. Check:

    - GC's CGL: primary coverage for property damage, products/
      completed operations, additional insured obligations to
      the project owner
    - GC's umbrella/excess: follow-form analysis for CGL coverage
    - GC's professional liability (if the GC provided any design
      or engineering services)
    - Subcontractor default insurance (if the GC carries SDI)
    - Builder's risk (if still in effect or if the claim relates
      to the construction period)
    - Contractor's pollution liability
    - Any wrap-up / OCIP policy (if the project was wrapped)

    For each subcontractor, identify whether the GC is an
    additional insured under their policies and whether the
    subcontractors' policies are primary and noncontributory.

    Map the full coverage picture including all parties and all
    policies.
    ```
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Cyber incident with regulatory exposure">
    A client suffers a data breach affecting customer records. The incident triggers regulatory investigation and potential class-action litigation.

    ```
    Identify all policies that may respond to this data breach
    and its consequences. Check:

    - Cyber liability: first-party (breach response, notification,
      forensics, business interruption) and third-party (privacy
      liability, regulatory defense and fines)
    - CGL: personal and advertising injury coverage for privacy
      violations (noting that many CGL policies now exclude
      cyber, but check the specific language)
    - D&O: if the breach leads to shareholder derivative suits
      or regulatory actions against officers
    - E&O/Professional liability: if the breach arose from
      professional services delivered to a client
    - Crime/fidelity: if the breach involved social engineering,
      funds transfer fraud, or employee dishonesty
    - Media liability: if the breach involved unauthorized
      publication of private information
    - Umbrella/excess: for any primary layer that is implicated

    Pay special attention to the interplay between the cyber policy
    and the CGL. Many brokers assume the cyber policy is the only
    response, but depending on the CGL's exclusionary language,
    there may be overlapping coverage worth pursuing.
    ```
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Employer liability / workplace claim">
    An employee is seriously injured on the job. The claim involves workers' comp, a potential third-party suit, and OSHA involvement.

    ```
    Identify all policies that may respond to this workplace
    injury. Check:

    - Workers' compensation: Part A (statutory benefits)
    - Employers' liability: Part B (common-law claims by the
      employee, cross-suits, third-party-over claims)
    - CGL: third-party claims arising from the same incident
      (injury to non-employees, property damage)
    - Umbrella/excess: follow-form to employers' liability and
      GL
    - Auto: if the injury involved a company vehicle or occurred
      during vehicle operation
    - EPLI: if the claim involves allegations of unsafe working
      conditions tied to discriminatory practices or retaliation
    - Professional liability: if the injury occurred during the
      delivery of professional services to a client
    - D&O: if OSHA enforcement actions result in personal
      liability for officers

    Also check whether any contractual indemnification obligations
    exist that would shift defense or indemnity obligations to
    a third party.
    ```
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Common missed-policy scenarios

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="The umbrella that doesn't follow form">
    A claim exceeds the primary limits, and the umbrella is expected to provide excess coverage. But the umbrella has an exclusion for the specific type of loss that doesn't appear in the primary. This creates a gap in the tower. Loading the full program into the Claims Analysis report catches this because Qumis compares the exclusions across every layer.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="The crime policy on a social engineering loss">
    A client falls victim to a social engineering scam (fraudulent wire transfer instructions). The cyber policy may or may not cover social engineering depending on the form. The crime policy, which many brokers don't think to check, often has a social engineering endorsement or computer fraud coverage that responds. Loading both policies ensures you don't miss it.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="The CGL on a cyber claim">
    Newer CGL policies often exclude cyber-related claims, but older forms or forms without a specific cyber exclusion may provide personal and advertising injury coverage for privacy violations. The distinction depends on the specific exclusionary language. Loading the CGL alongside the cyber policy into the analysis catches any overlap.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="The D&O on a regulatory investigation">
    When a regulatory agency investigates the client (OSHA, SEC, EPA, state AG), the D&O policy may provide defense cost coverage for officers and directors even if the primary target is the company. This is frequently overlooked because the claim doesn't look like a traditional D&O claim. Loading the full program surfaces it.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="The pollution policy on an indoor air quality claim">
    Standard CGL policies exclude pollution, but standalone environmental or pollution liability policies often cover indoor air quality, mold, Legionella, and similar claims that the GL excludes. If the client has a separate pollution policy, it's worth checking every time an environmental or air quality issue arises.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Measuring success

| Metric | What to track | Target |
|---|---|---|
| Full-program scans completed | Percentage of claims where the entire program was loaded and analyzed, not just the obvious policy | 100% for claims above $25K; 80%+ for all claims |
| Additional responding policies identified | Number of claims where the analysis found coverage under a policy that was not initially identified | Track quarterly; even one per quarter represents significant value |
| Total coverage identified per claim | Sum of available limits across all responding policies vs. the limit of the single "obvious" policy | Consistently higher than single-policy analysis |
| Third-party / additional insured findings | Number of claims where AI or third-party coverage was identified and pursued | Track per claim |
| Reporting completeness | Percentage of claims where all potentially responding carriers received timely notice | 100% |
| Coverage tower accuracy | Percentage of tower maps that were consistent with carrier behavior (correct attachment, correct follow-form) | 95%+ |

<Note>
  The cost of missing a responding policy is measured in unrecovered claims dollars. A single missed policy on a single claim can cost the client more than a year of Qumis licensing. Make the full-program load your default on every claim, regardless of how straightforward it appears.
</Note>
