## The strategy

A claim denial is not the end of the conversation. It's the beginning of a different one. Carriers deny claims for many reasons: some legitimate, some based on overly aggressive readings of exclusions, some based on factual misunderstandings, and some based on legal positions that don't hold up under the applicable law. The broker's job is to evaluate the denial, determine whether it's well-founded, and if it isn't, push back with a rebuttal that the carrier has to take seriously.

Most brokers handle denials informally. They call the examiner, argue the point verbally, and hope the carrier reconsiders. Occasionally they write a letter that restates their position without citing the specific policy language or legal authority that supports it. These approaches fail more often than they should because they don't force the carrier to engage with the merits.

Qumis changes the rebuttal process by giving you the tools to build a legally grounded, citation-heavy response that addresses the carrier's denial point by point. The Chat within the Claims Analysis report lets you research legal precedent through iterative dialogue, testing different arguments, exploring case law, and refining your position before generating the final rebuttal letter. The output is a document that reads like it came from coverage counsel, backed by policy language citations and case authority from the applicable jurisdiction.

This doesn't guarantee the carrier reverses the denial. But it dramatically increases the likelihood that they do, and if they don't, it gives the client's coverage counsel a running start if litigation becomes necessary.

## Prerequisites

<Info>
  This playbook is most effective when you've already completed a [Coverage Analysis](/playbooks/claims/coverage-analysis) on the claim before the denial arrived. If you have an existing Claims Analysis report for this claim, continue working in that same report. If you're starting fresh after receiving a denial, you'll need to build the coverage analysis first.
</Info>

Before starting, you should have:

- The carrier's denial letter (or reservation of rights letter with a coverage position)
- The relevant policy loaded in a Claims Analysis report
- The claim facts documented in the report
- An initial coverage analysis (ideally already completed before the denial)

## The Qumis workflow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Upload the denial letter to the Claims Analysis report">
    Add the carrier's denial letter to the existing Claims Analysis report. This ensures Qumis can reference the specific language and reasoning the carrier used, which you'll need to rebut point by point.

    If the denial came via email rather than a formal letter, copy the relevant text into the Chat along with the date, sender, and claim reference number.

    <Tip>
      Upload the full denial letter, not just the summary. Carriers often bury important qualifications, reservations, or alternative bases for denial in the middle or end of the letter. Qumis needs to see the complete document to identify every argument that needs addressing.
    </Tip>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Deconstruct the carrier's denial">
    Ask the report's Chat to break down the denial into its component arguments. Most denials rest on one to three specific bases. You need to identify each one precisely before you can rebut them.

    **Denial deconstruction prompt:**
    ```
    I've uploaded the carrier's denial letter for this claim.
    Analyze the denial and identify:

    1. EACH BASIS FOR DENIAL: List every distinct reason the
       carrier gives for denying coverage. For each, quote the
       specific language from the denial letter and identify the
       policy provision the carrier is relying on.

    2. FACTUAL ASSERTIONS: Identify any factual claims the carrier
       makes in the denial. Note whether each factual assertion
       is accurate, inaccurate, or incomplete based on the claim
       facts we've documented.

    3. LEGAL POSITIONS: Identify any legal positions the carrier
       takes (e.g., "the pollution exclusion bars coverage for
       this type of claim"). Note whether the carrier cites any
       case law or statutory authority, and if so, whether the
       citations are relevant and correctly applied.

    4. POLICY LANGUAGE RELIED UPON: For each basis of denial,
       quote the exact policy language the carrier references.
       Compare it against the actual policy uploaded in this
       report. Flag any misquotations, omissions, or instances
       where the carrier is reading the language out of context.

    5. WHAT THE CARRIER DIDN'T ADDRESS: Identify any provisions
       that support coverage (insuring agreement, exceptions to
       exclusions, favorable definitions) that the carrier did
       not discuss in the denial. These are often the strongest
       rebuttal points because the carrier failed to address them.

    Be precise. The deconstruction is the foundation of the
    rebuttal.
    ```
  </Step>

  <Step title="Research legal precedent through iterative dialogue">
    This is the core of the rebuttal process. For each basis of denial, use Legal Search within the report's Chat to find case law in the applicable jurisdiction that supports the insured's coverage position. The iterative dialogue allows you to test arguments, refine search queries, and build your legal foundation before writing the rebuttal.

    **Initial legal research prompt:**
    ```
    The carrier denied coverage on the following basis:
    [Quote the carrier's first basis for denial]

    The carrier relies on the following policy language:
    [Quote the policy provision]

    In [state], find case law that supports the insured's position
    that this denial is incorrect. Specifically, search for:

    1. Cases where courts interpreted the same or similar policy
       language and found coverage under comparable facts.

    2. Cases establishing the applicable rules of policy
       interpretation in [state] (e.g., contra proferentem,
       reasonable expectations doctrine, strict construction
       of exclusions against the insurer).

    3. Cases addressing the specific coverage issue (e.g., cases
       holding that [describe the specific issue, like "indoor
       mold does not constitute a 'pollutant' under the standard
       CGL pollution exclusion"]).

    4. Any regulatory guidance, department of insurance bulletins,
       or statutory provisions in [state] that are relevant.

    For each case, provide: case name, court, year, key holding,
    and the specific language from the opinion that supports
    the insured's position.
    ```

    **Then iterate.** The first search gives you a foundation. Follow up with targeted queries that deepen the research:

    **Narrowing on the strongest argument:**
    ```
    The [case name] decision looks directly on point. Are there
    any subsequent decisions in [state] that followed or
    distinguished this holding? Has the ruling been cited by
    other courts in the jurisdiction? I want to make sure this
    authority is still good law and hasn't been narrowed.
    ```

    **Addressing the carrier's likely counter-argument:**
    ```
    The carrier might argue that [describe the anticipated
    counter-argument, e.g., "the insured had prior knowledge of
    the condition, which takes the claim outside the 'occurrence'
    definition"]. Find cases in [state] that address this counter-
    argument and hold that [describe the favorable position, e.g.,
    "an insured's knowledge of a defective condition does not
    negate the fortuitous nature of the resulting damage where
    the insured did not intend or expect the specific injury
    that occurred"].
    ```

    **Exploring a secondary argument:**
    ```
    Even if the carrier's primary exclusion argument has some
    merit, is there an exception to the exclusion that restores
    coverage? The policy includes [describe the exception].
    Find cases in [state] interpreting this exception and applying
    it to restore coverage in similar circumstances.
    ```

    <Tip>
      The iterative dialogue is what makes the rebuttal process powerful. Don't stop at the first round of legal research. Each follow-up question sharpens the argument. A typical rebuttal might require five to ten exchanges in the Chat before the legal position is fully developed. The entire dialogue is preserved in the report, creating a complete research trail.
    </Tip>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Test the rebuttal arguments for weaknesses">
    Before writing the rebuttal letter, ask the Chat to stress-test your arguments by identifying the strongest counter-arguments the carrier might raise.

    **Stress test prompt:**
    ```
    I'm preparing to rebut the carrier's denial on the following
    grounds:

    1. [Summarize your first rebuttal argument]
    2. [Summarize your second rebuttal argument]
    3. [Summarize your third rebuttal argument, if applicable]

    For each argument, identify:

    1. The strongest counter-argument the carrier could make in
       response.
    2. Any cases in [state] that support the carrier's position
       (not just the insured's).
    3. Whether there are any factual weaknesses in the insured's
       position that the carrier could exploit.

    I'd rather know the weaknesses now than be surprised later.
    For each weakness, suggest how to address it in the rebuttal
    letter (preemptively acknowledge and distinguish, or avoid
    and let the carrier raise it).
    ```

    <Note>
      This step is what separates a strong rebuttal from one that the carrier can easily dismiss. If you only research arguments that support your position, you'll be blindsided when the carrier responds with authority you didn't anticipate. Testing for weaknesses allows you to address them proactively or adjust your strategy.
    </Note>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Generate the rebuttal letter">
    With the legal research complete and the arguments tested, use the Chat to generate the rebuttal letter. The letter should address every basis of denial, cite policy language and case authority for each rebuttal point, and demand that the carrier reconsider.

    **Rebuttal letter generation prompt:**
    ```
    Based on the full analysis in this report, including the denial
    deconstruction, the legal research, and the stress test, generate
    a formal rebuttal letter to the carrier. Structure the letter
    as follows:

    HEADER:
    - Date
    - Carrier name and address
    - Re: [Insured name], [Policy number], [Claim number],
      [Date of loss]
    - "Response to Coverage Denial Dated [date of denial letter]"

    OPENING:
    State that you are writing on behalf of the insured to respond
    to the carrier's denial. Reference the denial letter by date
    and summarize the carrier's position in one to two sentences.
    State that the denial is not supported by the policy language
    or applicable law.

    FACTS:
    Provide a concise statement of the claim facts. Correct any
    factual inaccuracies from the denial letter. Note any facts
    the carrier omitted that are material to the coverage
    determination.

    REBUTTAL — ADDRESS EACH BASIS OF DENIAL:
    For each basis of denial identified in the deconstruction:
    1. State the carrier's position (quote the denial letter).
    2. Quote the actual policy language.
    3. Explain why the carrier's interpretation is incorrect.
    4. Cite the applicable case law from [state] that supports
       the insured's position, with specific holdings and quotes
       from the opinions.
    5. If the carrier failed to address a provision that supports
       coverage (an exception to an exclusion, a favorable
       definition, or an applicable endorsement), raise it here.

    LEGAL FRAMEWORK:
    Remind the carrier of the applicable rules of policy
    interpretation in [state]:
    - Ambiguity is construed against the insurer
    - The insurer bears the burden of proving an exclusion applies
    - Exclusions are strictly construed
    - The reasonable expectations of the insured are considered
    Cite the controlling authority for each rule.

    DEMAND:
    State clearly that the insured demands that the carrier
    reconsider and reverse the denial. Specify the relief sought:
    acceptance of coverage, payment of the claim, appointment of
    defense counsel, or whatever is appropriate. Set a response
    deadline (typically 30 days).

    BAD FAITH RESERVATION:
    If appropriate under [state] law, note that the insured
    reserves all rights and remedies, including claims for bad
    faith denial of coverage, and that the insured expects the
    carrier to reevaluate its position in light of the authority
    cited.

    CLOSING:
    Professional close requesting a written response by the
    deadline.

    The tone should be firm, professional, and authoritative.
    Not adversarial, not deferential. The letter should demonstrate
    that the insured's position is well-researched and that the
    carrier's denial will not go unchallenged.
    ```

    <Warning>
      The rebuttal letter is a significant document that can affect the client's legal rights. Before sending, review the letter carefully for accuracy. If the claim involves significant financial exposure (generally $100K+) or if bad faith is being reserved, have the client's coverage counsel review the letter before it's sent. The Qumis-generated letter provides the analytical foundation and draft language, which reduces counsel's review time, but counsel should approve the final version on high-stakes claims.
    </Warning>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Refine through additional dialogue if needed">
    Review the generated letter in the Chat. If any section needs strengthening, adjustment, or a different approach, continue the iterative dialogue.

    **Common refinement prompts:**

    **Strengthening a weak section:**
    ```
    The rebuttal on the pollution exclusion point needs more
    authority. Find additional cases in [state] where courts
    rejected the insurer's attempt to apply the absolute
    pollution exclusion to [specific substance or scenario].
    I want at least three strong citations for this point.
    ```

    **Adjusting tone:**
    ```
    The bad faith reservation paragraph is too aggressive for
    this carrier relationship. Rewrite it to preserve the
    reservation of rights without being adversarial. The goal
    is to signal seriousness without burning the bridge.
    ```

    **Adding a fallback position:**
    ```
    If the carrier won't reverse the full denial, I want the
    letter to include a fallback position requesting that the
    carrier at least accept a duty to defend while reserving
    on indemnity. Add a section that argues for the duty to
    defend under [state]'s broad duty-to-defend standard, citing
    the "four corners" or "eight corners" rule as applicable.
    ```

    **Simplifying for a non-legal audience:**
    ```
    The client wants to understand this letter before it's sent.
    Generate a one-page summary in plain language that explains:
    (1) what the carrier said, (2) why we disagree, (3) what
    the law says, and (4) what we're asking the carrier to do.
    The audience is a business owner, not a lawyer.
    ```
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Rebuttal strategies by denial type

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Exclusion-based denial">
    The carrier asserts that a specific exclusion bars coverage. This is the most common type of denial and often the most rebuttable.

    **Rebuttal approach:**
    1. Quote the exclusion exactly as written in the policy
    2. Analyze whether the facts actually trigger the exclusion (carriers sometimes apply exclusions too broadly)
    3. Check for exceptions to the exclusion that restore coverage
    4. Research how courts in the jurisdiction have interpreted the scope of this exclusion
    5. Argue that any ambiguity in the exclusion must be construed against the insurer
    6. If the exclusion was added by endorsement, check whether the endorsement was properly incorporated

    **Key Legal Search queries:**
    ```
    Cases in [state] narrowly construing the [specific exclusion]
    in a [policy type] and finding coverage despite the insurer's
    exclusion defense
    ```
    ```
    [State] cases holding that the insurer bears the burden of
    proving the applicability of a policy exclusion
    ```
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="'No occurrence' denial">
    The carrier argues that the underlying event doesn't constitute an "occurrence" as defined in the policy. Common in construction defect, environmental, and negligence cases.

    **Rebuttal approach:**
    1. Analyze the policy definition of "occurrence" (typically "an accident, including continuous or repeated exposure to substantially the same general harmful conditions")
    2. Apply the jurisdiction's test for what constitutes an "accident" (most states focus on whether the resulting damage was expected or intended, not whether the underlying act was intentional)
    3. Distinguish between the act and the consequence: the insured may have intentionally performed the work, but not intended the resulting damage
    4. Find jurisdiction-specific case law on the occurrence question for the relevant fact pattern

    **Key Legal Search queries:**
    ```
    [State] cases holding that [faulty workmanship / negligent
    acts / unintended consequences] constitute an "occurrence"
    under a CGL policy
    ```
    ```
    [State] standard for determining whether an event is an
    "accident" under the occurrence definition in a liability
    policy
    ```
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Late notice denial">
    The carrier denies based on the insured's failure to provide timely notice of the claim.

    **Rebuttal approach:**
    1. Check whether the jurisdiction requires the insurer to demonstrate prejudice from the late notice (many states do)
    2. If prejudice is required, argue that the carrier has not demonstrated actual prejudice
    3. If the notice was late, determine whether there was a reasonable explanation (the insured didn't know about the claim, the facts didn't suggest a claim until later)
    4. Check whether the policy's notice provision is a condition precedent or a covenant (different legal consequences)
    5. Research the jurisdiction's specific notice rules, which vary significantly

    **Key Legal Search queries:**
    ```
    [State] law on whether prejudice is required for late notice
    defense in liability insurance
    ```
    ```
    [State] cases finding that late notice did not excuse the
    insurer from coverage obligations where no actual prejudice
    was demonstrated
    ```
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Reservation of rights (partial denial)">
    The carrier accepts the claim subject to a reservation of rights, reserving the right to deny on certain grounds after investigation. This is a partial denial that requires a targeted response.

    **Rebuttal approach:**
    1. Identify each specific coverage issue the carrier is reserving on
    2. For each reserved issue, determine whether the carrier's position has merit
    3. Respond to the reservation with authority on each point, requesting that the carrier withdraw the reservation once it completes its investigation
    4. If the carrier's reservation triggers Cumis counsel rights in the jurisdiction (i.e., the insured's right to independent counsel when the carrier has a conflict of interest), assert those rights

    **Key Legal Search queries:**
    ```
    [State] law on the insured's right to independent counsel
    when the carrier issues a reservation of rights creating a
    conflict of interest
    ```
    ```
    [State] cases requiring the insurer to withdraw a reservation
    of rights when the reserved grounds lack factual or legal
    support
    ```
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Definitional denial">
    The carrier argues that the claim doesn't meet a key policy definition (e.g., the damage isn't "property damage," the act isn't a "professional service," the event isn't a "security incident").

    **Rebuttal approach:**
    1. Quote the definition exactly as written
    2. Apply the facts to each element of the definition
    3. If the definition is ambiguous as applied to the facts, invoke the contra proferentem doctrine
    4. Research how courts in the jurisdiction have interpreted the definition in similar contexts
    5. Check whether the carrier's interpretation would render the coverage illusory (a strong argument in many jurisdictions)

    **Key Legal Search queries:**
    ```
    [State] cases interpreting the definition of "[term]" in a
    [policy type] policy and finding that [fact pattern] falls
    within the definition
    ```
    ```
    [State] cases holding that a narrow interpretation of a
    policy definition would render the coverage illusory
    ```
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## After the rebuttal is sent

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Carrier reverses the denial">
    If the carrier reverses, confirm the reversal in writing and proceed with claims handling. Save the rebuttal and reversal in the Claims Analysis report as a record of the advocacy.

    Share the outcome with the client. This is one of the most powerful moments in a broker-client relationship. You challenged the carrier and won. The client will remember it.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Carrier partially reverses">
    If the carrier accepts on some grounds but maintains the denial on others, assess whether the remaining denial points are worth pursuing further. You may need a second round of rebuttal focused narrowly on the remaining issues, or it may be time to escalate to coverage counsel.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Carrier maintains the denial">
    If the carrier doesn't budge, the Qumis analysis becomes the foundation for the next step, which is typically engaging coverage counsel to file a formal coverage action or to negotiate at a higher level within the carrier. Share the complete Claims Analysis report with counsel. The legal research is already done, which significantly reduces counsel's ramp-up time and cost.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Carrier requests additional information">
    If the carrier asks for more facts or documentation before reconsidering, respond promptly. Update the Claims Analysis report with any new information and assess whether it changes the coverage analysis. If the new facts strengthen the position, update the rebuttal accordingly.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Measuring success

| Metric | What to track | Target |
|---|---|---|
| Rebuttal letters generated | Number of denials challenged with a Qumis-powered rebuttal | 100% of denials where the coverage analysis supports the insured |
| Rebuttal turnaround | Time from denial received to rebuttal letter sent | Under 5 business days for standard denials; under 10 for complex |
| Reversal rate | Percentage of rebuttals that result in full or partial reversal | 40–60% (varies by denial type and jurisdiction) |
| Dollars recovered | Claim dollars recovered through successful rebuttals | Track quarterly |
| Counsel cost savings | Reduction in coverage counsel fees when Qumis analysis is provided as rebuttal foundation | 30–50% reduction in outside counsel hours |
| Client awareness of advocacy | Percentage of clients who are informed of the rebuttal effort and outcome | 100% |
