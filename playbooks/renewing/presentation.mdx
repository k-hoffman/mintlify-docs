## The strategy

The renewal presentation is the moment where all your work becomes visible to the client. It's also the moment where most brokers underperform. The typical renewal presentation is a spreadsheet with carrier names, premiums, limits, and deductibles. The producer walks through it, points out the cheapest option, maybe mentions a few coverage differences, and asks the client to decide. The client, who doesn't read policy language for a living, picks based on price because that's the only dimension they can evaluate with confidence.

This is a failure of communication, not analysis. The broker may have done excellent work during marketing and negotiation, but if the presentation doesn't convey the depth of that work, the client can't appreciate it. Worse, a price-driven decision might leave the client with a weaker program than what they could have had.

Qumis changes the presentation by giving you a structured, citation-backed recommendation that the client can see and verify. Instead of "Carrier B has broader coverage," you show them the exact language in both quotes and explain what the difference means in practice. The client makes a decision based on understanding, not guesswork. And the presentation itself becomes a permanent record that documents your recommendation and the reasoning behind it.

## Prerequisites

<Info>
  This playbook follows the [Renewal Marketing](/playbooks/renewing/marketing) playbook. You should have a finalized options matrix with all carrier quotes analyzed, renewal objectives scored, and an internal recommendation ready before building the presentation.
</Info>

Before starting, you should have:

- A completed final options matrix from the marketing phase
- All carrier quotes uploaded and analyzed in the Document Vault
- The renewal strategy document (for framing)
- The expiring program for comparison
- A scheduled meeting with the client's decision-makers

## The Qumis workflow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Generate the renewal story arc">
    Before building the presentation, define the narrative. A strong renewal presentation tells a story: where the client started (the expiring program and its issues), what you did about it (the marketing process and objectives), what the market delivered (the options), and what you recommend (your pick and why).

    **Narrative framing prompt:**
    ```
    Based on the renewal strategy, the marketing results, and the
    final options matrix, generate a renewal presentation narrative
    that follows this arc:

    1. WHERE WE STARTED: Summarize the expiring program's key
       strengths and the issues identified in the renewal strategy.
       Reference specific stewardship findings, coverage gaps, and
       renewal objectives. This reminds the client why the renewal
       was approached the way it was.

    2. WHAT WE DID: Briefly describe the marketing approach. How
       many carriers were approached? What specific improvements
       were requested? What was the negotiation focus? This
       demonstrates the effort and strategy behind the results.

    3. WHAT THE MARKET DELIVERED: Summarize the quoting results at
       a high level. Were objectives met? Was the market receptive?
       Were there surprises (better terms than expected, unexpected
       restrictions, capacity challenges)?

    4. WHAT WE RECOMMEND: State the recommendation clearly. Which
       option and why? Frame it in terms of the client's stated
       priorities from the strategy kick-off meeting.

    Write as a presentation script. Each section should be three
    to five sentences. The tone is confident and advisory.
    ```
  </Step>

  <Step title="Build the executive summary">
    The executive summary is the first page the client sees and the page they'll reference after the meeting. It needs to work as a standalone document.

    **Executive summary prompt:**
    ```
    Generate a one-page renewal executive summary with:

    1. PROGRAM OVERVIEW: Current lines, carriers, total premium
       (expiring vs. proposed), and effective dates.

    2. KEY CHANGES FROM EXPIRING: The three to five most
       significant coverage improvements in the recommended
       program. For each, cite what the expiring said and what
       the renewal says.

    3. PREMIUM IMPACT: Total premium change (dollars and
       percentage). Break down by line if the changes vary
       significantly across lines.

    4. RECOMMENDATION: One clear sentence: "We recommend [option]
       because [top reason]."

    5. DECISION DEADLINE: When the client needs to decide to
       ensure binding before expiration.

    Format for a CFO or business owner who will scan before reading.
    Lead with the recommendation and the premium number.
    ```

    <Tip>
      The executive summary should be understandable in 60 seconds. If a client's CFO picks up this page without attending the meeting, they should know what you recommend, what it costs, and what's different from last year. Everything else in the presentation supports this page.
    </Tip>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Generate the detailed options comparison">
    This is the core analytical section. Use the final options matrix from marketing and enhance it for client consumption.

    **Detailed comparison prompt:**
    ```
    Using the final options matrix, generate a client-facing
    comparison of all renewal options. Structure as follows:

    FOR EACH LINE OF BUSINESS:

    1. OPTIONS TABLE: Show each carrier option with premium,
       limits, deductibles, and key terms in a scannable format.

    2. COVERAGE COMPARISON: For the three to five most significant
       coverage differences between options, show the exact
       language from each carrier's quote side by side. Explain
       in plain language what each difference means for the client.

    3. RENEWAL OBJECTIVES SCORECARD: For each renewal objective
       from the strategy, show which options met it and which
       didn't. Use clear indicators (Met / Partially Met / Not Met).

    4. VISUAL ANALYSIS: Use QumisScore and Visual Analysis to
       highlight the coverage quality differences at a glance.

    5. LINE-LEVEL RECOMMENDATION: For each line, state which
       option you recommend and the primary reason.

    THEN, AT THE PROGRAM LEVEL:

    6. TOTAL PROGRAM VIEW: Show the recommended program across
       all lines, total premium, and the net coverage position
       vs. expiring (improved, lateral, or reduced, and why).

    7. ALTERNATIVE PROGRAM STRUCTURES: If applicable, show one
       alternative configuration (e.g., "if the client wants to
       minimize cost, here's what the program looks like with
       higher retentions and different carrier selections, and
       here's what coverage they'd give up").

    Cite policy language for every coverage difference. The
    audience needs to see the evidence, not just the conclusion.
    ```
  </Step>

  <Step title="Add risk context for the recommendation">
    For the one to two coverage differences that matter most to the recommendation, add context that helps the client understand the real-world implications.

    Use Legal Search to find a relevant case or scenario:

    **Risk context prompt:**
    ```
    For the top coverage difference driving our recommendation
    ([describe the difference, e.g., "Carrier A's cyber policy
    excludes social engineering fraud; Carrier B covers it"]),
    find a relevant case, claim scenario, or industry example
    that illustrates why this difference matters. Present it in
    two to three sentences that a non-insurance audience can
    understand.
    ```

    This is the moment in the presentation where the client goes from "I see the difference" to "I understand why it matters." It's often the deciding factor between a price-driven decision and a value-driven one.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Prepare a what-you're-giving-up analysis for the cheaper option">
    Clients will always look at the cheapest option. Rather than hoping they don't, prepare a clear analysis of what the cost savings would actually mean in coverage terms.

    **Trade-off analysis prompt:**
    ```
    The client's lowest-cost option is [carrier/premium]. The
    recommended option is [carrier/premium], which is $[X] more.
    Generate a "cost of savings" analysis that shows:

    1. Every coverage difference between the two options where
       the cheaper option provides less protection.

    2. For each difference, quantify the potential exposure if
       possible (e.g., "the cheaper option has a $250K sublimit
       on water damage vs. $1M in the recommended option, meaning
       $750K of exposure shifts from the carrier to the client").

    3. A total risk assessment: on balance, is the premium
       savings worth the coverage concessions?

    Frame this as information for the client's decision, not as
    pressure. The client may have legitimate reasons to accept
    more risk for lower cost. Your job is to make sure they
    understand the trade-off.
    ```

    <Note>
      This analysis is one of the most valuable things you can do in a renewal presentation. It respects the client's autonomy while ensuring they make an informed decision. Clients remember the broker who showed them what they'd be giving up, whether they took the savings or not.
    </Note>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Generate the final presentation Report">
    Compile everything into a single Report that serves as both the meeting document and the permanent record.

    **Final report structure:**
    ```
    Generate the complete renewal presentation report with these
    sections in order:

    1. Executive Summary (one page)
    2. Renewal Story: Where We Started → What We Did → Market
       Results → Recommendation
    3. Detailed Options Comparison by Line of Business
       - Options table
       - Coverage comparison with citations
       - Renewal objectives scorecard
       - Visual Analysis / QumisScore
       - Line-level recommendation
    4. Total Program View with Recommended Configuration
    5. Alternative Program Structure (if applicable)
    6. Cost of Savings Analysis (cheapest vs. recommended)
    7. Risk Context for Key Coverage Differences
    8. Timeline and Next Steps (decision deadline, binding
       process, certificate issuance, policy delivery)

    The document should be comprehensive enough to stand alone
    but structured so that each section can be presented in
    two to three minutes during the meeting.
    ```
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Delivering the renewal presentation

The presentation meeting is where your analysis meets the client's decision. Here's the recommended flow.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Standard presentation (30–45 minutes)">
    **Meeting structure:**

    1. **Renewal story** (5 min): Recap the strategy, the marketing approach, and the market response. This sets the context and reminds the client of the journey.

    2. **Executive summary and recommendation** (5 min): State your recommendation and the premium impact up front. The client knows immediately where you're headed, and the rest of the meeting is about supporting the recommendation.

    3. **Line-by-line comparison** (15–20 min): Walk through each line, focusing on the two to three most significant coverage differences per line. Use the Qumis Report as a visual aid, pointing to specific citations when the client asks "are you sure?" or "why does that matter?"

    4. **Cost of savings analysis** (5 min): If the recommended option isn't the cheapest, walk through the trade-off analysis. Let the client see exactly what they'd gain or lose at each price point.

    5. **Risk context** (3 min): Share the one to two legal or real-world examples that illustrate why the key coverage differences matter.

    6. **Decision and next steps** (5 min): Confirm the client's selection, set the binding timeline, and outline post-binding activities (certificates, policy review, first stewardship meeting).
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Board or committee presentation (60 minutes)">
    For accounts where the decision requires board or risk committee approval, expand the presentation to include additional context.

    **Additional sections for board presentations:**

    - **Market conditions briefing** (5 min): Broader market context that explains why rates moved the way they did. Board members want to know whether the client's experience is in line with the market.

    - **Peer benchmarking** (5 min): If available, how does this program compare to similarly sized companies in the same industry? This helps board members calibrate.

    - **Three-year program trajectory** (5 min): Where was the program three years ago, where is it now, and where is it headed? This demonstrates long-term stewardship.

    - **Risk heat map** (5 min): A visual summary of the client's top exposures and how the recommended program addresses each one. Use Qumis findings to populate the heat map with specific policy references.

    <Tip>
      Board members and risk committees are less interested in policy language than in business impact. Translate every coverage finding into a business consequence: "This exclusion means a $2M exposure that's currently uninsured" resonates more than "this exclusion is broader than the standard ISO form."
    </Tip>
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Virtual presentation">
    Virtual renewal presentations require tighter pacing and more visual emphasis because you can't read the room as easily.

    **Virtual presentation adjustments:**

    - **Share the Report on screen** rather than referencing a printed document. Walk the client through it visually, highlighting key sections.

    - **Lead with the executive summary** and pause for reactions before moving deeper. In virtual settings, silence often means confusion, so check in more frequently.

    - **Use the QumisScore and Visual Analysis** as the anchor visuals. These are easier to digest on screen than dense text comparisons.

    - **Send the full Report 15 minutes before the meeting** so the client can have it open alongside the screen share. This lets them flip ahead if they want to, and ensures they have the document if the connection drops.

    - **Record the meeting** (with permission) so decision-makers who couldn't attend can review the recommendation with the full context.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Handling client objections during the presentation

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="'Why is it more expensive?'">
    This is the most common objection. Use the Qumis analysis to separate price from value.

    **Response framework:**
    > "The premium increase breaks down into two components. First, there's market-driven rate change: carriers across this class are adjusting rates by [X]%, so part of the increase is market movement that would apply regardless of carrier. Second, part of the increase reflects the coverage improvements we requested in our renewal strategy, specifically [reference the improvements]. The alternative is to keep the expiring coverage and terms, which would reduce the increase to approximately [X]%, but you'd still have [reference the gaps identified in the strategy]. Here's the exact comparison."

    Then show the cost-of-savings analysis you prepared.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="'My friend's broker got them a better rate'">
    Anecdotal comparisons are common and usually unhelpful because the programs aren't comparable. Use Qumis to reframe the conversation.

    **Response framework:**
    > "Rates vary significantly based on the specific program structure, limits, retentions, and coverage breadth. What often looks like a better rate is actually a different product: lower limits, higher retentions, or narrower coverage. Without seeing their actual policy language, it's impossible to compare. What I can show you is exactly what your program covers, what each option costs, and what you'd be giving up at a lower price point. That way you're comparing apples to apples on your own program."
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="'Can you go back and get one more quote?'">
    Sometimes the client wants more options. Assess whether the request is productive or a delay tactic.

    **Response framework:**
    > "We can absolutely approach additional markets. Let me share what that looks like: we submitted to [X] carriers, [Y] quoted, and [Z] declined. The carriers who declined did so because [reason]. Adding another carrier at this point would take approximately [X] weeks, which puts us [close to/past] the expiration date. My recommendation is [option], and here's why I'm confident in it. But if you'd like us to test one more market, we can do that as long as we keep the current best option held. Which specific concern would the additional quote need to address?"
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="'Let's just stay with the incumbent and keep it simple'">
    When the client wants to default to the path of least resistance, use the Qumis analysis to show them what they'd be leaving on the table.

    **Response framework:**
    > "The incumbent is a legitimate option, and we've included them in the analysis. Here's what the comparison shows: [reference specific findings]. Staying with the incumbent means [describe what stays the same and what doesn't improve]. The recommended option provides [specific improvements] for [premium difference]. I want you to make the decision that's right for your business. Let me walk you through the two options side by side so you can see exactly what the difference is."

    Then use the Qumis Report to walk through the head-to-head comparison.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="'I need to think about it'">
    Respect the client's process, but make sure they have everything they need to decide and that the timeline is clear.

    **Response framework:**
    > "Absolutely. You have the full analysis in the report I've shared. The key decision points are on pages [X] and [Y]. I'd just flag that the quotes expire on [date] and we need to bind by [date] to ensure no lapse. Can we schedule a follow-up call for [date] to answer any remaining questions and finalize?"
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Post-decision workflow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Document the decision and rationale">
    After the client makes their selection, save the final presentation Report in the Vault with a note documenting the decision, who made it, and the stated rationale. This is your E&O documentation and your baseline for the next renewal cycle.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Communicate binding instructions to carriers">
    Send binding instructions to the selected carrier immediately. Reference the specific terms, endorsements, and premium from the finalized quote to prevent any discrepancies at the binder stage.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Run the binding review">
    When the binder arrives, follow the [Binding and Policy Review](/playbooks/broking/binding-review) playbook to verify that everything matches what was presented and agreed upon.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Schedule the first stewardship meeting">
    Before the renewal cycle closes, schedule the first stewardship review for 90 days into the new policy period. This maintains continuity and starts the next renewal cycle's intelligence gathering immediately.

    <Tip>
      The best time to schedule the first stewardship meeting is during the renewal presentation meeting itself. The client is engaged, they see the value of ongoing analysis, and putting it on the calendar before the meeting ends ensures it actually happens.
    </Tip>
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Measuring success

| Metric | What to track | Target |
|---|---|---|
| Presentations with Qumis Reports | Percentage of renewal presentations that included a Qumis-powered options comparison and recommendation | 100% |
| Client decision at first presentation | Percentage of clients who make a decision during or immediately after the presentation | 70%+ |
| Recommendation acceptance rate | Percentage of clients who selected the broker's recommended option | 65–75% |
| Presentation-to-bind time | Days from presentation meeting to confirmed bind | Under 10 days |
| Client satisfaction with process | Feedback on clarity of the presentation and confidence in the decision | Track via direct feedback or NPS |
| Renewal retention rate | Percentage of accounts successfully renewed | 90%+ overall; 95%+ for accounts with full lifecycle Qumis usage |

A Qumis-powered presentation doesn't just win the renewal. It sets the standard for what the client expects from their broker going forward. That standard is your moat.
</Note>
