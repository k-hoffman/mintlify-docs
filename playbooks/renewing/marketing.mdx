## The strategy

Renewal marketing operates under constraints that new business placement doesn't. You have an incumbent relationship to manage, a ticking clock, a client with expectations shaped by last year's program, and carriers who know whether the account has been shopped before. The margin for error is smaller and the consequences of a poorly run process are immediate: a carrier non-renewal, a missed deadline, or a subpar result that makes the client question your value.

The advantage you have at renewal is information. You know the program. You know the client. You've spent 12 months servicing the account. The question is whether you use that information effectively when you go back to market.

Qumis makes the difference by converting your accumulated intelligence into a precise, data-driven marketing process. Your submissions reference specific coverage gaps you've identified. Your negotiations cite the exact policy language you want improved. Your carrier conversations are grounded in analysis, not anecdote. The result is better quotes, faster turnaround, and terms that actually reflect what you asked for.

Before starting, you should have:

- A completed renewal strategy document with prioritized recommendations
- The client's full program in Document Vault
- Updated exposure data from the client (loss runs, SOVs, revenue figures, headcount, vehicle schedules, etc.)
- A target carrier list and a clear plan for which lines to remarket vs. negotiate with incumbents
- Timeline: begin marketing at 90 days before expiration

## The Qumis workflow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Build line-by-line submission packages">
    For each line being remarketed, use Qumis to build a submission package that goes beyond the standard ACORD application. This follows the same approach as the [Marketing the Account](/playbooks/broking/marketing-account) broking playbook, but with renewal-specific intelligence layered in.

    **Renewal submission narrative prompt:**
    ```
    Based on the renewal strategy and the analysis of the expiring
    program, generate a submission narrative for the [specific line
    of business] renewal. The narrative should:

    1. Describe the insured's operations and risk profile, noting
       any material changes from the prior year (growth, new
       operations, acquisitions, divestitures).

    2. Summarize the expiring terms: carrier, limits, retention,
       key endorsements, and premium.

    3. Describe the renewal objectives: what specific coverage
       improvements, term changes, or structural modifications
       are being requested and why.

    4. Present the account favorably: highlight positive risk
       characteristics including loss experience, risk management
       practices, and any improvements made during the current
       policy period.

    5. Specify any required endorsements, minimum terms, or
       deal-breaker provisions.

    Write as a professional broker-to-underwriter submission cover
    letter. Keep to one page per line of business.
    ```

    <Tip>
      Reference the specific coverage findings from your Qumis analysis when describing renewal objectives. Instead of "we'd like to explore broader coverage," write "the expiring policy's pollution exclusion at Section I.2.f includes language that was recently interpreted by the [X] Circuit to exclude coverage for indoor air quality claims. We need the renewal to include the limited pollution exception endorsed under CG 21 65."
    </Tip>
  </Step>

  <Step title="Prepare incumbent negotiation points">
    For lines staying with the incumbent carrier, use Qumis to build a structured negotiation brief. This is where your year of servicing intelligence becomes leverage.

    **Incumbent negotiation prompt:**
    ```
    Based on the expiring program and the renewal strategy, build
    a negotiation brief for the incumbent carrier on [line of
    business]. Include:

    1. ACCOUNT VALUE SUMMARY: Premium history, years with the
       carrier, loss ratio, and any positive claims experience
       that supports retention.

    2. TERM IMPROVEMENTS REQUESTED: List every coverage
       modification, endorsement addition, or limit change being
       requested. For each, cite the specific expiring policy
       language that needs to change and explain why. Reference
       stewardship findings, coverage questions, or contract
       requirements that surfaced the need.

    3. RATE EXPECTATIONS: Based on market conditions for this class,
       what rate outcome is reasonable? Is this a line where you
       expect flat, a decrease, or a modest increase? Justify with
       market context.

    4. COMPETITIVE CONTEXT: Without bluffing, indicate whether the
       line is being competitively marketed. If it is, frame the
       incumbent negotiation as their opportunity to retain the
       account with improved terms rather than lose it on price
       alone.

    5. DECISION CRITERIA: What factors will determine whether the
       account stays with the incumbent? (Terms, price, service,
       willingness to negotiate manuscript changes.)

    Write as an internal strategy document for the broker's use
    in carrier conversations.
    ```

  </Step>

  <Step title="Analyze incoming quotes against renewal objectives">
    As quotes come back from carriers, upload each one to the Document Vault and run a targeted analysis that measures the quote against your specific renewal objectives, not just against the expiring.

    **Renewal quote analysis prompt:**
    ```
    Compare this carrier quote against both the expiring policy and
    the renewal objectives defined in our strategy. For each renewal
    objective, determine:

    1. MET: The quote satisfies the objective. Cite the specific
       language in the quote that addresses it.

    2. PARTIALLY MET: The quote addresses the objective but not
       fully. Explain the gap between what was requested and what
       was offered.

    3. NOT MET: The quote does not address this objective. Note
       whether this is a deal-breaker or a negotiable item.

    4. UNEXPECTED: The quote includes terms or provisions not
       present in the expiring that were not part of our renewal
       objectives. Flag whether these are improvements or
       restrictions.

    Also compare the quote against the expiring on all standard
    dimensions: limits, deductibles, coverage grants, exclusions,
    endorsements, conditions, and premium. Cite all differences.
    ```

    This analysis is different from a standard quote comparison because it measures against your stated objectives, not just against the status quo. It answers the question "did the market give us what we asked for?" rather than just "what changed from last year?"
  </Step>

  <Step title="Run iterative negotiation rounds">
    Use the gap analysis from each quote to structure your counter-negotiation. For every objective marked "partially met" or "not met," go back to the carrier with the specific language you need.

    **Counter-negotiation prompt:**
    ```
    Based on the quote analysis, generate a counter-negotiation
    request for [carrier name]. For each renewal objective that was
    not fully met, draft the specific ask:

    1. State what was requested (cite the renewal strategy).
    2. State what was offered (cite the quote language).
    3. State what needs to change, with the specific language or
       terms you need to see in the revised quote.

    Prioritize the asks: lead with the items that are most
    important to the client and most likely to be negotiable.
    Separate must-haves from nice-to-haves.

    Format as a professional email to the underwriter.
    ```

  </Step>

  <Step title="Build the final options matrix">
    Once all quotes are finalized (including any negotiated revisions), run a comprehensive comparison that will feed directly into the renewal presentation.

    **Final options matrix prompt:**
    ```
    Produce a final renewal options matrix comparing all carrier
    quotes against the expiring program. For each option, show:

    1. CARRIER AND PREMIUM: Total premium and change from expiring
       (dollars and percentage).

    2. RENEWAL OBJECTIVES SCORECARD: For each renewal objective
       from the strategy, indicate whether the option meets it
       (Met / Partially Met / Not Met).

    3. COVERAGE IMPROVEMENTS OVER EXPIRING: Specific terms that
       are better than what the client has today.

    4. COVERAGE CONCESSIONS FROM EXPIRING: Specific terms that are
       worse than what the client has today.

    5. NET ASSESSMENT: On balance, does this option represent an
       improvement, a lateral move, or a step back from the
       expiring program? Consider both coverage and cost.

    6. RECOMMENDATION: Which option do you recommend and why?
       State the top three reasons.

    Cite policy language for every finding. Structure the output
    for direct inclusion in a client presentation.
    ```
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Managing the marketing timeline

Renewal marketing operates under time pressure. Here's how to keep the process on track.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Submission phase">
    All submissions should go out within the first two weeks of this phase. The Qumis-powered submission narratives are pre-built from the strategy work, so assembly is fast.

    **Checklist:**
    - [ ] All submission packages assembled with Qumis-generated narratives
    - [ ] Incumbent negotiation emails sent
    - [ ] Quote deadlines communicated to all carriers (target: 45–60 days pre-renewal)
    - [ ] Client kept informed of which markets are seeing the submission
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Quote collection and analysis">
    As quotes arrive, upload them immediately and run the renewal objective analysis. Don't wait until all quotes are in to start analyzing the early ones.

    **Checklist:**
    - [ ] Each quote uploaded to Vault and analyzed within 24 hours of receipt
    - [ ] Gap analysis completed for each quote against renewal objectives
    - [ ] Counter-negotiation requests sent for any partially met or unmet objectives
    - [ ] Quote tracker updated and shared with the producer
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Negotiation and finalization">
    This is where the back-and-forth happens. Use the Qumis gap analysis to keep negotiations focused and specific.

    **Checklist:**
    - [ ] All counter-negotiations completed
    - [ ] Revised quotes uploaded and re-analyzed
    - [ ] Final options matrix generated
    - [ ] Recommendation finalized internally before presenting to client
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Presentation preparation">
    Build the renewal presentation (next playbook) using the final options matrix and the full body of Qumis analysis.

    **Checklist:**
    - [ ] Final options matrix completed with all carrier quotes
    - [ ] Renewal presentation built using the Proposal Generator
    - [ ] Client presentation meeting scheduled
    - [ ] All options confirmed as available (carrier quotes not expired)
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## When the market doesn't cooperate

Not every renewal goes as planned. Here's how to use Qumis when the marketing results are challenging.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Carrier non-renewal or declination">
    If the incumbent or a target market declines, use Qumis to quickly assess alternative program structures.

    **Alternative structure prompt:**
    ```
    The [carrier/line] has been non-renewed or declined. Based on
    the remaining available options, analyze: (1) whether any
    current quotes can be restructured to fill the gap, (2) what
    alternative program structures are possible (higher retentions,
    different tower architecture, surplus lines options), and
    (3) what coverage concessions the client would need to accept
    under each alternative. Present with pros, cons, and your
    recommendation.
    ```
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="All quotes significantly above expiring">
    When the market has hardened and every option is more expensive, use Qumis to help the client understand what they're paying for.

    **Rate increase context prompt:**
    ```
    All renewal quotes for [line of business] represent a
    significant premium increase over expiring. Analyze the
    quotes to determine: (1) whether any of the increased cost
    is offset by improved coverage terms, (2) what retention or
    limit adjustments could bring the premium closer to the
    expiring level and what coverage impact those changes would
    have, (3) rank the available options by value (coverage
    quality per premium dollar), not just by price. Help the
    client understand whether they're paying more for the same
    thing or paying more for something better.
    ```
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Quotes missing key renewal objectives">
    When no carrier meets a critical renewal objective, you need to reassess the ask or find creative solutions.

    **Unmet objective analysis prompt:**
    ```
    No carrier quote has met the renewal objective of [describe
    the unmet objective]. Analyze: (1) whether any quote comes
    close and could be negotiated further, (2) whether the
    objective can be met through a different mechanism (separate
    policy, endorsement from a different carrier, contractual
    risk transfer), and (3) what the client's actual risk
    exposure is if this objective remains unmet. Include any
    relevant Legal Search context on how this gap has played
    out in claims scenarios.
    ```
  </Tab>
</Tabs>
